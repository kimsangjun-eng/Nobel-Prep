Thomson's Three Experiments
Experiment 1

No Field Applied

Cathode rays travel in a perfectly straight line from cathode to anode, hitting the center of the screen.

Experiment 2

Electric Field Applied

Rays bend toward the positive plate — proving they carry negative charge. This ruled out light (which has no charge).

Experiment 3

Fields Balanced

By balancing E and B fields so the beam travels straight, Thomson calculated the charge-to-mass ratio (e/m) of the electron.

Key Discovery

Thomson proved that cathode rays are streams of negatively charged particles (electrons) — the same regardless of the cathode material or gas used. The electron's mass was ~1/1837 that of a hydrogen atom. This was the first subatomic particle ever discovered, disproving the idea that the atom was indivisible.

Experimental Timeline
1

Magnetic Deflection → Charges the Beam

Thomson bent the cathode rays using a magnet. He connected the bent rays to an electrometer, detecting negative charge — proving the rays are electrically charged particles, not waves or light.

2

Electric Deflection → Negative Charge Confirmed

Between two charged plates, rays bent toward the positive plate and away from the negative plate. This definitively proved negative charge. (Hertz had failed this experiment because his tube had too much residual gas — Thomson improved the vacuum.)

3

Balanced Fields → e/m Ratio Measured

Thomson applied both electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields simultaneously, adjusted until the beam traveled straight. At balance: eE = evB, so velocity v = E/B. He then used the electric deflection alone to find e/m, getting the same value regardless of cathode material or gas used.

Why This Matters
🧩

Atoms Are Divisible

For 2000 years, the atom was thought to be the smallest unit of matter. Thomson's electron proved atoms have internal structure — a revolutionary idea.

🔋

Universal Particle

The same e/m ratio appeared for every cathode material and gas tested. The electron is a fundamental constituent of all matter — not just one element.

🍮

Plum Pudding Model

Thomson proposed electrons were embedded in a diffuse positive "pudding" — the Plum Pudding Model. Later disproved by Rutherford's gold foil experiment.

🏆

Nobel Prize 1906

Thomson won the Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery of the electron — the first fundamental subatomic particle ever identified.
