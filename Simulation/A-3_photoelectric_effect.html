Photoelectric Effect

Light hits a metal surface — will electrons be ejected? Frequency matters, not intensity.

A-3
Einstein (1905) explained the photoelectric effect using Planck's quantum idea: light travels as photons, each with energy E = hν. An electron is ejected only if a photon's energy exceeds the metal's work function (Φ). The excess becomes kinetic energy of the ejected electron: KE = hν − Φ. Increasing intensity sends more photons but does not increase each photon's energy.

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation
Energy conservation for the photoelectric effect
KE = hν − Φ hν = photon energy  (h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)
Φ = work function — minimum energy to eject an electron (metal-dependent)
KE = kinetic energy of ejected electron (= 0 if hν < Φ)

Threshold frequency:  ν₀ = Φ / h  — below this, no electrons are ejected regardless of intensity
